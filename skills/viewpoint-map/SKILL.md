# Viewpoint Map

Map real disagreements after establishing a shared evidence baseline.

## Rules

- Derive viewpoints from the dispute. Do not force a predetermined ideological checklist or reduce the issue to two sides.
- Ground each material viewpoint in named people, institutions, publications, texts, movements, affected groups, or decision makers.
- Treat every representative skeptically. Institutional prestige, outsider status, ideology, funding, and popularity do not determine factual accuracy.
- Steelman a serious view before critique, then audit its strongest factual premises using the same standards applied to opponents.
- Separate factual, causal, legal, strategic, moral, religious, economic, and historical disagreement.
- Explain the mechanism each viewpoint believes connects events, institutions, incentives, and outcomes.
- Identify ownership, funding, membership, audience, political constraints, career incentives, and material interests when relevant. Do not infer motive merely from affiliation.
- Apply motive language symmetrically. Do not describe one camp as public-spirited and another as self-interested without evidence.
- Include local, international, heterodox, dissident, or low-power views when they possess relevant evidence or bear material consequences, not as decorative balance.
- Finish the evidence audit in the current task. Genuine unavailable evidence may yield an indeterminate factual premise; obtainable central evidence may not be deferred.

## Workflow

1. Define the issue, boundary, time, geography, institutions, affected groups, and key terms.
2. Establish shared facts and important unknowns from primary and specialist evidence.
3. Identify the real disputes and the actors advancing them.
4. For each viewpoint record:
   - Named representatives and source material.
   - Core factual and moral claims.
   - Causal and policy theory.
   - Evidence cited and underlying methodology.
   - Institutional position, incentives, funding, and audience.
   - Strongest point and hard case.
   - Blind spots, contradictions, and what would change it.
5. Fact-check the strongest factual claims with source-neutral standards.
6. Compare competing mechanisms, prerequisites, implementation, enforcement, tradeoffs, distribution, and failure modes.
7. Test contested labels or classifications with explicit definitions and comparable cases.
8. Review whether any viewpoint received easier evidentiary treatment or asymmetrical motive assumptions.

## Output

- Issue and evidence baseline.
- Main factual, causal, legal, strategic, moral, and historical disputes.
- Named viewpoint map with claims, evidence, incentives, strengths, and blind spots.
- Steelman and evidence-based critique.
- Claim checks for each major viewpoint.
- Policy-theory comparison when relevant.
- Classification consistency test when relevant.
- Media and institutional framing notes.
- Missing evidence and genuine indeterminacy.
- Questions that would distinguish the competing accounts.

For broad Evidence Audit Projects, use the `world-research` schemas, source audits, extractions, and generated gate.
