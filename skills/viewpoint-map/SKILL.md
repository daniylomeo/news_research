# Viewpoint Map

Use this skill to compare interpretations and narratives without collapsing them into simple partisan teams.

## Purpose

The goal is to help the user understand how different actors see an issue, what evidence each uses, what incentives shape them, what each side gets right, and where each account becomes weak, exaggerated, or unsupported.

## Rules

- Do not present "both sides" as if every issue has only two sides.
- Do not treat neutrality as splitting the difference.
- Separate factual disputes from moral, strategic, ideological, religious, economic, or geopolitical disagreements.
- Steelman serious viewpoints before critiquing them.
- Include serious dissident, heterodox, local, and non-mainstream arguments when they are relevant to the issue, even if they are not prominent in corporate media.
- Label advocacy and commentary clearly.
- Include power, funding, ownership, institutional role, and audience incentives where relevant.
- For NGOs, nonprofits, think tanks, and advocacy groups, verify their methods, data, sourcing, conflicts, and major claims; list named disclosed funders above the relevant reporting threshold when available, especially government and corporate funders. Do not accept generic funding summaries as enough; if donor names are not public, say so and treat opacity as part of the viewpoint's incentive profile.
- Keep factual confidence separate from sympathy or antipathy toward a viewpoint.
- Include non-U.S. and local viewpoints when the issue is not strictly U.S.-domestic.
- Preserve enough source trail to show where each viewpoint was identified.
- After mapping viewpoints, fact-check the major factual claims made by each viewpoint. Do not stop at describing rhetoric.
- When a viewpoint relies on scientific studies, statistics, NGO reports, or think-tank work, summarize the underlying methodology and limits rather than only quoting the headline conclusion.
- When the issue involves policy choices, present competing policy philosophies as serious arguments, not stereotypes. Include libertarian, Austrian/free-market, public-choice, industrial-policy, labor, environmental, localist, civil-liberties, and technocratic views when relevant.
- Explain how each viewpoint thinks the system works. Do not only state who benefits or what they want; explain the mechanism, incentives, and tradeoffs they emphasize.
- For historical debates, explain the historical events, movements, doctrines, parties, documents, and institutions that each viewpoint invokes before comparing them.
- For debates involving named places, sacred sites, borders, legal bodies, armed groups, treaties, parties, or institutions, explain the relevant background before using the name.
- For viewpoints based on democracy indices, NGO ratings, institutional scorecards, or expert rankings, inspect the methodology, funding, governance, critiques, and primary evidence before treating the rating as support.
- For contested labels or typologies, require symmetrical application. If a viewpoint calls one actor fascist, authoritarian, imperial, or democratic, test other comparable actors against the same standard.
- Do not create generic placeholder camps. Viewpoints should be represented through real actors, institutions, texts, movements, or identifiable schools of thought with actual arguments and evidence.
- If a viewpoint cannot be anchored to named representatives or identifiable texts/institutions, label it as incomplete or cut it. Do not fill space with "some critics," "business leaders," "market-oriented economists," "the left/right," or similar placeholders.

## Workflow

1. Define the issue and time frame.
2. Build a shared issue frame before mapping sides: core facts, timeline, definitions, institutions, incentives, affected groups, data limits, and why reasonable people disagree.
3. Build an explanatory baseline: define the key institutions, technical terms, economic mechanisms, legal tools, and jobs or social roles that each viewpoint is arguing about.
4. For history-heavy topics, build a historical baseline: explain the relevant chronology, named events, movements, doctrines, primary texts, and scholarly disputes before mapping modern interpretations.
5. Identify actors: governments, parties, agencies, corporations, NGOs, unions, local communities, religious groups, experts, journalists, dissidents, movements, foreign states, and affected people.
6. Identify narrative sources:
   - Primary statements from actors.
   - Corporate media reports and editorials.
   - Ideological magazines, think tanks, policy shops, advocacy groups.
   - Local reporting and community accounts.
   - Academic or historical interpretations.
   - Social media narratives when relevant and verifiable.
7. For each viewpoint, capture:
   - Main public voices or institutions representing it.
   - Core claim or moral frame.
   - Causal theory: how this viewpoint thinks events happened or policy works.
   - Policy theory: what rule or institutional arrangement it prefers and why.
   - Evidence cited.
   - Concrete examples or cases used by the viewpoint.
   - Method and source quality behind the claim.
   - If evidence is a rating/index/scorecard, its methodology, funding/governance, and critiques.
   - Interests, incentives, funding, and institutional position.
   - Ideological tradition or worldview.
   - Strongest point.
   - Weakest point or blind spot.
   - What evidence would change or complicate it.
8. Look for missing viewpoints, especially affected local communities, dissidents, smaller ideological traditions, and actors with weak media access.
9. Steelman before critique: present the best version of each serious argument in a form its informed supporter would recognize.
10. For policy recommendations, compare options:
   - Mechanism of action.
   - Prerequisites and implementation steps.
   - Expected benefits.
   - Costs and tradeoffs.
   - Enforcement problems and failure modes.
   - Historical attempts or analogues.
   - Implementation risks.
   - Best objection from opposing schools of thought.
   - Evidence that would support or falsify the policy theory.
11. For classification debates, build a rubric and consistency check:
   - Define the label and threshold.
   - Score or assess each actor with evidence notes.
   - Identify cases that challenge the conclusion.
   - Explain why similar-looking cases are or are not classified the same way.
12. Extract and fact-check the strongest factual claims from each major viewpoint.
13. Compare narratives against the fact base from primary and specialist sources.
14. Add a short adversarial review: which viewpoint may be underrepresented, over-weighted, or misunderstood.
15. For broad Evidence Audit Projects using this skill inside `world-research`, run that skill's preflight gate before delivery. Treat failures about generic viewpoint camps, missing claim checks, or missing extraction rows as blocking.

## Viewpoint Labels

Use specific labels when evidence supports them. Examples:

- Liberal institutionalist
- Conservative institutionalist
- Libertarian
- Socialist or social democratic
- Labor-oriented
- Business or market-oriented
- Nationalist
- Populist
- Realist foreign-policy
- Neoconservative or liberal interventionist
- Anti-imperialist or anti-colonial
- Religious/traditionalist
- Environmentalist
- Feminist
- Civil-liberties oriented
- Austrian/free-market
- Public-choice/institutional-skeptical
- Industrial-policy/developmentalist
- Security/state-capacity oriented
- Local/community affected
- Technocratic or expert-managerial
- Dissident/whistleblower

If a label is uncertain, say so and explain the basis.

## Output Format

- **Issue:** one-sentence framing.
- **Issue Context:** definitions, timeline, institutions, incentives, data limits, and affected groups.
- **Explanatory Baseline:** the key machinery, institutions, terms, jobs, and mechanisms each side is arguing about.
- **Historical Baseline:** for history-heavy topics, explain necessary events, movements, doctrines, documents, and scholarly disputes before modern comparison.
- **Shared Facts:** points most credible sources agree on.
- **Main Disputes:** factual, causal, moral, legal, strategic, or historical.
- **Viewpoint Table:** actor/view, core claim, causal theory, policy theory, evidence, incentives, strengths, blind spots.
- **Steelman And Critique:** best version of each serious argument, then what evidence supports, narrows, or contradicts it.
- **Policy Debate:** competing policy options, prerequisites, mechanisms, implementation steps, tradeoffs, enforcement problems, historical attempts, failure modes, and strongest objections.
- **Claim Check:** strongest factual claims from each viewpoint, with status, confidence, and method notes for key studies/statistics/reports.
- **Rubric Consistency Check:** for contested labels, show whether the standard is applied evenly across comparable actors.
- **Media Narrative Notes:** how corporate, partisan, state-affiliated, and independent outlets frame the issue.
- **Best Questions:** questions that cut through slogans and test the competing accounts.
- **Blind Spots:** viewpoints or evidence that may still be missing.
