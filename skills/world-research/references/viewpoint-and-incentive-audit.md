# Viewpoint and Incentive Audit

Derive relevant viewpoints from the actual dispute. Do not force every project through a fixed ideological list.

For each material viewpoint:

- Name current or historically relevant people, institutions, publications, texts, movements, affected groups, or decision makers.
- State its factual claims, causal theory, values, preferred policy, and evidence.
- Audit its strongest factual premises using the same standards applied to every other viewpoint.
- Identify institutional position, ownership, funding, membership, audience, political constraints, career incentives, and material interests when relevant.
- Separate evidence-based criticism from rhetoric, moral judgment, and strategic framing.
- State what the viewpoint explains well, what it misses, and what would change it.

Do not assume public institutions are public-spirited, firms are self-interested, activists are altruistic, or dissidents are courageous. Apply motive language symmetrically and require evidence for motive claims.

Include local, international, heterodox, or low-power perspectives when they possess relevant evidence or bear consequences. Do not include them merely to create balance.

For policy disputes, compare mechanisms, prerequisites, implementation, enforcement, costs, distribution, failure modes, and historical analogues. The relevant alternatives may be legal, technical, institutional, local, security-oriented, market-oriented, labor-oriented, environmental, or otherwise topic-specific.
