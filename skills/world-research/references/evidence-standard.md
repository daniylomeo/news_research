## Governing rule

Treat every source as a container of claims and evidence. Never treat reputation, institutional status, peer review, ideology, or outsider status as proof. Evaluate only the evidence relevant to the specific claim being tested.

Prefer the term `audited for claim X` over `credible source`. A source can be accurate about one date, opaque about a method, and unreliable about causation at the same time.

## Evidence chain

For every load-bearing claim, record:

- Exact proposition and scope.
- Source and claim-specific audit scope.
- Raw evidence: quotation, record, observation, data, result, or finding.
- Pinpoint locator: page, table, row, docket, timestamp, code section, or named web-page section.
- Provenance: who created the evidence, when, for what purpose, and how it reached the source.
- Measurement: unit, denominator, categories, outcomes, instruments, and classification rules.
- Method: how raw evidence became a result or conclusion.
- Reproduction: calculation, classification, record matching, or other check attempted and its result.
- Assumptions, exclusions, missing data, uncertainty, and sensitivity.
- Alternative explanations and strongest counterevidence.
- Claim fit: whether the public claim matches the evidence's magnitude, population, time, geography, and uncertainty.
- Verdict, confidence basis, and what evidence would change the verdict.

Do not substitute phrases such as `methodology appears sound`, `reputable source`, `official data`, or `peer reviewed` for these fields.

## Source-neutral evaluation

Use source identity to identify incentives and likely failure modes, not to predetermine the verdict.

- Official records may be authoritative for legal text, administrative action, or reported totals while remaining self-interested or incomplete about performance and causation.
- Peer review establishes that a paper passed a journal process, not that its design identifies the public claim.
- Journalism may contain unique interviews or documents but must be traced to underlying evidence where possible.
- Advocacy and industry research may contain high-quality data despite incentives; reproduce the data and test the inference.
- Dissident or ideological work may reveal ignored evidence or may use selective cases; audit both possibilities without stigma shortcuts.
- Primary testimony is close to an event but may be mistaken, strategic, incomplete, or contradicted.
- Secondary scholarship can outperform a primary source when it transparently synthesizes a larger record; inspect its selection and method.

Do not assign a single global credibility score. Record claim-specific strengths and limitations.

## Verdicts and confidence

Use these claim statuses:

- `supported`: the audited evidence supports the claim as stated.
- `mostly_supported`: the core survives but wording, scope, magnitude, or certainty needs qualification.
- `mixed`: material evidence points in different directions or different parts receive different verdicts.
- `weakened`: evidence materially reduces the claim's plausibility without fully contradicting it.
- `contradicted`: audited evidence is inconsistent with the claim as stated.
- `indeterminate`: the available record cannot distinguish the important alternatives.
- `unauditable`: necessary evidence is unavailable or the claim cannot be operationalized.
- `context`: relevant background that does not itself decide a load-bearing claim.
- `pending`: research is incomplete and blocks delivery when load-bearing.

Use `high`, `moderate`, `low`, or `indeterminate` confidence. Justify confidence through evidence proximity, method, reproducibility, convergence, counterevidence, and uncertainty. Never justify it through prestige alone.

## Completion versus indeterminacy

An indeterminate result can be complete when:

- The necessary record is genuinely unavailable, proprietary, sealed, destroyed, future, or nonexistent.
- Reasonably obtainable alternatives were checked.
- The competing explanations are stated.
- The evidentiary consequence is explained.
- `what_would_change` identifies the missing kind of evidence without promising another Codex pass.

An incomplete result is not complete when an ordinary public source, full paper, dataset documentation, filing, transcript, or relevant rebuttal remains reasonably obtainable.
