# Research and Journalism Standards

Use these references when auditing, revising, or tightening the research skills.

## Existing AI Skill Starting Points

- `fdaudens/ai-journalism-skills` includes a public `fact-checker` skill with useful structure: claim extraction, source research, credibility assessment, quality rating, and report generation. Keep its useful claim-ledger discipline, but adapt away from a single numeric credibility score when qualitative source incentives matter more.
- `Research & Investigation Claude Code Skill` focuses on technical research and best-practice discovery. It is useful as a workflow model, but not sufficient for political, historical, media, or source-bias research.

## Professional Standards To Preserve

- Society of Professional Journalists Code of Ethics: verify before publishing, use original sources whenever possible, provide context, identify sources clearly, label advocacy/commentary, and correct mistakes.
- International Fact-Checking Network Code of Principles: nonpartisanship and fairness, transparency of sources, transparency of funding and organization, transparency of methodology, and open corrections.
- Verification Handbook and Verification Handbook 2: verify digital content, assess source credibility, corroborate open-source information, and document methodology.
- Reuters Trust Principles: independence, integrity, reliability, and freedom from bias as stated institutional standards.

## Practical Implications For These Skills

- Research should not be limited to "answer only what the user asked." Treat the user's question as the entry point, then build enough surrounding issue knowledge to understand the answer: definitions, timeline, institutions, actors, incentives, data limits, mechanisms, affected populations, and policy tradeoffs.
- A briefing should explain how the issue works. Do not only name technical systems, institutions, job categories, beneficiaries, or cost bearers; explain what they are, why they matter, how money/power/resources move through them, and what can go wrong.
- Core evidence belongs in the first pass. Do not conclude first and list central source review, case audits, PDF extraction, data checks, or rebuttal analysis as future work. If the work is central and cannot be completed, narrow the research boundary and lower the conclusion.
- Every major section should stand on its own. Avoid bare lists of claims without concrete examples, citations, mechanisms, and counterarguments. An audit project or synthesis should not contain "summary filler" sections that rely on some other section to be rigorous.
- Every important claim should be traceable to evidence.
- Substantial research should include an audit trail: key searches, source paths, dates checked, and inaccessible or unresolved sources.
- Important quotes should be checked against original transcripts, recordings, or documents where possible, with surrounding context.
- Major conclusions should receive a contradiction search and adversarial self-review before final output.
- Source labels should describe type, method, proximity, ownership, funding, viewpoint, and limits instead of declaring a source simply "biased" or "unbiased."
- Primary sources outrank secondary summaries, but primary sources still need context and incentive analysis.
- Peer review is not a guarantee of sound science. When relying on scientific literature, inspect the study itself: research question, design, sample, exposure/intervention, comparator, outcomes, time period, model/specification, effect sizes, uncertainty, confounders, missing data, data/code availability, conflicts, limitations, journal/publisher quality, replication, and retraction concerns. Do not substitute an abstract, press release, or headline conclusion for method appraisal.
- NGOs, nonprofits, think tanks, and advocacy groups should be treated as institutions with incentives and claims requiring verification. When they are cited materially, inspect their methods, data, sourcing, conflicts, and whether conclusions follow from evidence; identify major disclosed funders, especially government and corporate funders; and note when funding or methodology is unavailable or opaque.
- For controversial topics, establish facts first, then include a debate/commentary section that identifies mainstream, institutional, ideological, local, international, and dissident voices. Steelman serious arguments before critiquing them, then fact-check the strongest claims from each viewpoint. Debate/commentary sections must reflect real debates, not generic camps; use named actors, institutions, texts, movements, or identifiable schools of thought.
- Historical research must meet the same standard as present-day fact-checking. When using history or analogy, provide real context: chronology, geography, definitions, mechanisms, social conditions, labor effects, political conflict, environmental consequences, distribution of gains and losses, major scholarly disputes, and limits of the comparison.
- Historical names are not explanations. When a briefing names events, movements, doctrines, texts, parties, laws, or institutions, explain what they were, what happened, what arguments they made, why they mattered, and how historians dispute them. Do not ask the user to supply background knowledge that the research should provide.
- Proper nouns are not explanations. When naming a place, sacred site, border, legal body, armed group, treaty, party, or institution that matters to the argument, explain what it is, why it matters, and how it affects the dispute.
- Primary ideological texts should be read as arguments, not used as reputation markers. Summarize their premises, enemy images, theory of state/society, justification for power, and relationship to practice.
- Policy conclusions should be argued, not asserted. Present mechanisms, expected benefits, costs, tradeoffs, implementation problems, and serious counterarguments from relevant schools of thought, including libertarian, Austrian/free-market, public-choice, localist, labor, environmental, technocratic, and industrial-policy perspectives.
- Complex conflicts should not receive toy policy proposals. Present policy paths as contested frameworks with prerequisites, implementation steps, enforcement problems, historical attempts, failure modes, tradeoffs, and objections. Do not imply that a few sentences solve an intractable issue.
- Democracy ratings, freedom indices, human-rights scorecards, corruption indices, and similar products must be audited like NGO or think-tank claims. Inspect methodology, coding, funding, governance, ideological/institutional incentives, critiques, uncertainty, and primary evidence before using them as support.
- Scoring tables and typologies are dangerous if unsupported. Use them only with an explicit rubric, evidence notes, and a consistency check. Apply labels symmetrically across favored and disfavored cases, and explain why similar conduct receives different classifications.
- When the question alleges hidden purposes, covert action, propaganda, laundering, institutional misconduct, or euphemistic program language, the first pass must include a representative primary-record audit. Sample programs, cases, countries, or grants before drawing conclusions; do not defer this as a later improvement if it is central to the question.
- When the question depends on concrete projects, local implementation, facilities, subsidies, permits, contracts, or regulatory decisions, the first pass should include a representative case audit using primary local records where possible. National claims should be tested against ground-level implementation.
- Corporate media should be used in two ways: as reporting to verify when well-sourced, and as narrative evidence showing how institutions frame events.
- A good briefing should make it easy to see what is known, what is contested, and what remains unverified.
- A good workflow should fail closed. If the draft is shallow, generic, source-weak, or over-aggregated, the agent should mark it as a failed draft and keep revising rather than summarize it confidently.
- Broad comparisons require decomposition. Before answering "which place/system is better," split the analysis by geography, class, health, age, family status, policy regime, and plausible counterexample cases.
- Use a preflight gate before delivery. The gate should catch deferred central evidence, generic viewpoint camps, weak aggregators, unsupported rubrics, missing counterexamples, and sections that are mostly compressed assertions.
